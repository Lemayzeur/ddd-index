1
Spatial poverty structure
Poverty is spatially persistent. Mississippi Delta counties consistently rank highest (17–28%). COVID temporarily masked trends but poverty resumed its pre-2020 trajectory by 2022.
2
Federal dollars vs poverty need
HHS ($1,498B) and Social Security dominate — but these are entitlement transfers, not targeted investment. HUD and Agriculture remain underweight relative to regional need, particularly in the Delta.
3
Fiscal neglect is measurable
Using Poverty Z − Spending Z, the entire Mississippi Delta region shows positive misalignment (underfunded), while most of Virginia shows negative scores (overfunded relative to need).
4
Model accuracy
Gradient Boosting achieves mean R²=0.838 across rolling windows. Median income and public assistance rate are the strongest predictors. GB outperforms ResNet and GNN on 7 of 11 metrics.
Policy implication: A reallocation of even 5% from direct payments toward competitive grants in underfunded Delta counties would represent a structural shift toward reducing persistent poverty rather than maintaining it.

Key Findings & Conclusions
What the data
tells policymakers

Five study objectives. Five clear answers.

1
Objective 1 — Map spatiotemporal poverty structure
Poverty is geographically concentrated and temporally persistent. Delta counties cluster above 20% consistently. Within Appalachia, Eastern Kentucky and southern West Virginia form a sub-cluster of extreme poverty that aggregate state averages obscure.
2
Objective 2 — Measure whether federal dollars flow to highest-need counties
They do not — consistently. Every Delta state averages positive misalignment scores across all five agencies. SSA shows the most severe negative trend (−0.96 in Tennessee), suggesting Social Security flows increasingly to non-poor counties.
3
Objective 3 — Pinpoint fiscally neglected counties
Tunica County, MS (+3.20 score, 27.7% poverty) is the most underfunded. The entire Delta corridor contains the highest density of underfunded counties. These are not data gaps — they are policy gaps.
4
Objective 4 — Build a county-year panel dataset
Successfully constructed a 9,245-row panel linking 23 ACS indicators with 14M+ transaction records across 624 counties and 10 years — the first dataset to combine USASpending agency-level breakdowns with Census indicators at this granularity.
5
Objective 5 — Forecast county-level poverty trajectories
Gradient Boosting achieves R²=0.838 (rolling) and 0.821 (CV). Post-2020 degradation (R² to 0.767) confirms COVID was a structural break — the model's declining performance is itself a finding, not a failure.
The single most actionable finding: 84.2% of federal assistance is direct payments. Only 7.9% is competitive grants. Shifting 5% of the portfolio — roughly $19B over 10 years — from transfers to targeted grants in the 50 most underfunded Delta counties would represent the largest structural investment in these communities since the Great Society programs of the 1960s.
